# Doctrinal Sources Bibliography

A comprehensive bibliography of all Russian military doctrine sources cited in the Colonel General project, organized by category.

---

## Official Russian State Documents

**Russian Federation.** *Military Doctrine of the Russian Federation* (Военная доктрина Российской Федерации). Approved by Presidential Decree, 25 December 2014. English translations available via Carnegie Endowment for International Peace and RAND Corporation.
Defines Russia's threat perceptions (NATO expansion as primary external military threat), the conflict spectrum (armed incident through large-scale war), and the broadened definition of military risks to include information and communication technologies used for military-political purposes against Russian sovereignty.

**Russian Federation.** *National Security Strategy of the Russian Federation* (Стратегия национальной безопасности Российской Федерации). Presidential Decree No. 683, 31 December 2015. English translations via Russia Matters (Harvard Belfer Center) and IEEE.
Frames the post-Cold War international order as designed to constrain Russian power. Explicitly attributes the Ukraine crisis to US and EU policies. Identifies color revolutions and democracy promotion as instruments of Western regime change threatening Russian security.

**Russian Federation.** *Basic Principles of State Policy of the Russian Federation on Nuclear Deterrence* (Основы государственной политики Российской Федерации в области ядерного сдерживания). Executive Order signed by President Putin, 2 June 2020. English text: kremlin.ru; analysis: Arms Control Association, July 2020.
The first public codification of Russia's nuclear employment conditions. Defines four thresholds: (1) reliable data on ballistic missile launch against Russian territory or allies; (2) nuclear or WMD use against Russia or allies; (3) attack on critical sites undermining nuclear response capability; (4) conventional aggression threatening the very existence of the state. Describes the nuclear posture as "defensive by nature."

## Russian Military Journal Articles

**Gerasimov, Valery V.** "Ценность науки в предвидении" ("The Value of Science Is in the Foresight"). *Voyenno-Promyshlennyy Kurier* (VPK), 26 February 2013. English translation: *Military Review*, January-February 2016.
The Chief of the General Staff's analysis of the Arab Spring and color revolutions, assessing the ratio of non-military to military measures at approximately 4:1. Widely mischaracterized in Western media as the "Gerasimov Doctrine" prescribing Russian hybrid warfare. The actual text presents this ratio as a description of Western methods that Russia must defend against.

**Chekinov, S.G. and S.A. Bogdanov.** "О характере и содержании войны нового поколения" ("The Nature and Content of a New-Generation War"). *Voyennaya Mysl* (Military Thought), No. 10, 2013. English translation via USNI.
Argues that the re-division of territory and markets is now achieved through "indirect approaches and the employment of nonmilitary means, including information confrontation." Part of a series of 13 articles published in *Voyennaya Mysl* between 2010 and 2017 that shaped Russian thinking on new-generation warfare.

**Chekinov, S.G. and S.A. Bogdanov.** "Asymmetric Actions to Ensure Russia's Military Security." *Voyennaya Mysl*, No. 3, 2010.
Early articulation of information confrontation as a potentially decisive main effort rather than a supporting function. Establishes the conceptual framework for treating the information domain as a permanent battlefield with no peacetime distinction.

## Foundational Soviet/Russian Military Theory

**Svechin, Alexander A.** *Strategy* (Стратегия). Moscow, 1927. English translation: East View Information Services, 1992. Edited by Kent D. Lee.
Originated the concept of operational art (оперативное искусство) as the intermediate level between tactics and strategy. Argued for the distinction between wars of attrition and wars of annihilation, and that strategic planning must account for economic, political, and social factors alongside military ones. Remains foundational reading at the Military Academy of the General Staff.

**Triandafillov, Vladimir K.** *The Nature of the Operations of Modern Armies* (Характер операций современных армий). Moscow, 1929. English translation: Frank Cass Soviet Study of War series.
The founding text of Soviet deep operations theory. Developed the concept of simultaneous effects across the full depth of the operational area — striking command, logistics, reserves, and communications simultaneously rather than sequentially. Triandafillov died in 1931 but his operational concepts were codified in the 1936 Field Regulations (PU-36).

**Isserson, Georgii S.** *The Evolution of Operational Art* (Эволюция оперативного искусства). Moscow, 1932; revised 1937. English translation: Combat Studies Institute Press, Fort Leavenworth, Kansas. Translated by Bruce W. Menning.
Extended Triandafillov's work with the argument that "the continuous front of modern war demands operations in depth executed by successive echelons." Developed the theory of deep battle into a comprehensive operational framework. Isserson was one of the first Soviet theorists to analyze the German blitzkrieg operations of 1939-1940.

**Druzhinin, V.V. and D.S. Kontorov.** *Concept, Algorithm, Decision: Decision Making and Automation* (Идея, алгоритм, решение). USSR Ministry of Defense, 1972. English translation: U.S. Air Force Soviet Military Thought Series, Vol. 6.
The foundational text on multi-factor military decision-making and the Correlation of Forces and Means (COFM) methodology. Establishes that military decision-making must integrate quantitative analysis across military, economic, political, and technological dimensions. Also contains early formulations of reflexive control theory, arguing that control of the adversary's decision process derives from "a profound knowledge of the state of his forces, military doctrine, objectives, and personal qualities of his executive personnel."

**Lefebvre, Vladimir A.** *Algebra of Conscience: A Comparative Analysis of Western and Soviet Ethical Systems*. Dordrecht: D. Reidel Publishing, 1982. Revised edition: Kluwer Academic Publishers, 2001.
Lefebvre originated the concept of reflexive control (рефлексивное управление) in 1965. This work provides the formal mathematical and philosophical framework for understanding how decision-making can be shaped by controlling the information and context available to the decision-maker. The concept has no direct Western military equivalent and is central to Russian information confrontation doctrine.

## Western Analysis of Russian Doctrine

**Bartles, Charles K.** "Getting Gerasimov Right." *Military Review*, January-February 2016.
The essential corrective to the widely propagated "Gerasimov Doctrine" myth. Demonstrates that Gerasimov's 2013 article was an analysis of how the West uses non-military means (color revolutions, democracy promotion) to achieve regime change — a threat assessment, not a prescriptive Russian strategy for hybrid warfare. Required reading for anyone interpreting Russian military thinking.

**Thomas, Timothy L.** "Russia's Reflexive Control Theory and the Military." *The Journal of Slavic Military Studies*, Vol. 17, No. 2, 2004, pp. 237-256.
The definitive Western analysis of reflexive control. Defines it as "a means of conveying to a partner or an opponent specially prepared information to incline him to voluntarily make the predetermined decision desired by the initiator of the action." Traces the concept from Lefebvre's original formulation through its integration into Soviet and Russian military doctrine.

**Thomas, Timothy L.** *The Chekinov-Bogdanov Commentaries of 2010-2017*. Foreign Military Studies Office (FMSO), March 2021.
Comprehensive analysis of all 13 Chekinov and Bogdanov articles published in *Voyennaya Mysl* over seven years. Tracks the evolution of Russian thinking on new-generation warfare, information confrontation, and the integration of military and non-military measures. Essential for understanding the doctrinal debates within the Russian military establishment.

**Reach, Clint, Vikram Kilambi, and Mark Cozad.** *Russian Assessments and Applications of the Correlation of Forces and Means*. RAND Corporation, RR-4235-OSD, 2020.
Confirms that modern Russian COFM methodology uses "qualimetric methods and expert elicitation" encompassing military, economic, political, informational, and technological factors — far more comprehensive than simple order-of-battle comparisons. Demonstrates that COFM remains the foundational analytical framework taught at the Military Academy of the General Staff.

**Glantz, David M.** *Soviet Military Deception in the Second World War*. London: Frank Cass, 1989. Routledge reprint, 2006.
The authoritative history of maskirovka (military deception) in practice. Documents how deception was integral to all Soviet operations at every level — strategic, operational, and tactical — not optional or supplementary. Demonstrates the four principles of Soviet deception: activity, naturalness, diversity, and continuity. Essential for understanding why Russian military planning instinctively includes concealment, disinformation, feints, and demonstrations as standard components.

**Grau, Lester W. and Charles K. Bartles.** *The Russian Way of War: Force Structure, Tactics, and Modernization of the Russian Ground Forces*. Foreign Military Studies Office (FMSO), 2016.
Comprehensive analysis of Russian military force structure, organizational culture, and career tracks. Critical for understanding the General Staff's institutional structure, including the separation between GRU intelligence career pipelines and General Staff operational officer career paths. Provides the factual basis for the persona's career track and institutional knowledge.

**Ven Bruusgaard, Kristin.** "Russian Nuclear Strategy and Conventional Inferiority." *Journal of Strategic Studies*, 2020. Winner of the Amos Perlmutter Prize.
Challenges the Western "escalate to de-escalate" narrative by demonstrating that improved Russian conventional capabilities will delay, not accelerate, nuclear employment. Argues that Russian nuclear strategy is more defensive and restrained than commonly portrayed in Western analysis. See also her earlier piece, "The Myth of Russia's Lowered Nuclear Threshold," *War on the Rocks*, 22 September 2017.

**Oliker, Olga and Andrey Baklitskiy.** "The Nuclear Posture Review and Russian 'De-Escalation': A Dangerous Solution to a Nonexistent Problem." *War on the Rocks*, 20 February 2018.
Argues that "the evidence of a dropped threshold for Russian nuclear employment is weak" and that the Western "escalate to de-escalate" concept has been projected onto Russian doctrine without adequate textual support. Important corrective for anyone building Red team personas that involve nuclear decision-making.

**Rid, Thomas.** *Active Measures: The Secret History of Disinformation and Political Warfare*. New York: Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2020.
Comprehensive history of Soviet and Russian active measures (активные мероприятия) — political warfare, covert action, disinformation, and influence operations as standard instruments of state power. Documents the institutional continuity from Soviet-era operations through contemporary Russian information warfare.

**Shultz, Richard H. and Roy Godson.** *Dezinformatsia: Active Measures in Soviet Strategy*. Washington: Pergamon-Brassey's, 1984.
The foundational Western analysis of Soviet active measures during the Cold War. Establishes the doctrinal framework for understanding how Russia views political warfare, proxy operations, and deniable action as natural extensions of statecraft rather than extraordinary measures.

**Kofman, Michael.** Various analyses. Center for Naval Analyses (CNA) and *War on the Rocks*, ongoing.
Leading Western analyst of Russian military operations, particularly the Syria intervention (2015-present). Analyses cover the Russian expeditionary model (limited footprint with significant firepower), PMC employment as deniable force multipliers, combat testing of new systems (Kalibr cruise missiles, advanced electronic warfare), and reconciliation centers as components of information warfare. Essential for understanding contemporary Russian operational practice.

**Glantz, David M.** *The Evolution of Soviet Operational Art, 1927-1991: The Documentary Basis*. Two volumes. London: Frank Cass, 1995.
Primary-source General Staff Academy documents tracing the development of Soviet operational art from Svechin and Triandafillov through World War II and the Cold War. Provides the documentary evidence base for understanding how deep operations, operational art, and campaign planning evolved within Russian military thought.

**Lider, Julian.** "The Correlation of World Forces: The Soviet Concept." *Journal of Peace Research*, Vol. 17, No. 2, 1980.
Early Western analysis of the Soviet COFM methodology as applied beyond purely military calculations. Documents how Soviet strategists integrated economic, political, and social factors into force correlation assessments, establishing the intellectual foundation that persists in modern Russian military analysis.

**Galeotti, Mark.** "I'm Sorry for Creating the 'Gerasimov Doctrine.'" *Foreign Policy*, 5 March 2018.
The Western analyst who originally coined the term "Gerasimov Doctrine" publicly retracted it, acknowledging that the label misrepresented what Gerasimov actually wrote. Important for understanding how Western analytical frameworks can distort understanding of Russian military thinking — precisely the mirror-imaging problem the Colonel General persona is designed to counteract.
